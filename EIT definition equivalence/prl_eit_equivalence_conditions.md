# PRL Strategy: General Equivalence Conditions for Electromagnetically Induced Transparency

## From Dark-State Equivalence to Response-Zero Universality

## 1. Central objective

The aim of this work is not merely to exhibit one unusual transparency window without a dark state. The broader objective is to reformulate electromagnetically induced transparency (EIT) as a universal property of an observable input--output response.

The proposed conceptual shift is

\[
\boxed{
\text{EIT is not fundamentally defined by a dark state or by CPT.}
}
\]

Instead,

\[
\boxed{
\text{EIT is a control-induced coherent cancellation in an observable optical response.}
}
\]

A dark state and coherent population trapping (CPT) are then interpreted as one important realization of this cancellation, rather than as its universal definition.

The PRL-level claim should therefore be:

> **A regular control-induced transfer zero, generated by a nonvanishing coherent sector response, is the general response-theoretic condition for EIT. Dark-state formation and CPT become equivalent to this condition only in the ideal single-excited-state \(\Lambda\) limit.**

---

## 2. Declared scope

The theory is formulated for:

1. finite-dimensional systems;
2. Markovian GKSL dynamics;
3. a stationary rotating frame;
4. weak-probe linear response;
5. passive optical response without gain;
6. a well-defined source and readout channel;
7. a physically specified long-lived coherence sector \(S\);
8. regular observation frequencies away from poles.

The theory does not automatically include:

- strong-probe nonlinearities;
- non-Markovian memory;
- continuum or propagation instabilities;
- gain media;
- many-body collective effects;
- time-dependent feedback or Floquet driving.

The intended claim is therefore:

> **A general equivalence theorem for finite-dimensional Markovian weak-probe EIT.**

---

## 3. The observable response and the sector cut

Let the probe susceptibility or normalized response be

\[
\chi_{\mathrm{full}}(\omega).
\]

Choose a physically meaningful long-lived coherence sector \(S\). Define the sector-cut response

\[
\chi_{\mathrm{cut}}^{(S)}(\omega)
\]

by removing only the couplings into and out of sector \(S\), while keeping fixed:

- the direct optical block;
- the probe source;
- the detector;
- the zeroth-order steady state;
- all uncoupled dissipative terms;
- the observation protocol.

The master sector response is

\[
\boxed{
R_S(\omega)
=
\chi_{\mathrm{full}}(\omega)
-
\chi_{\mathrm{cut}}^{(S)}(\omega).
}
\]

This is the response generated through sector \(S\).

The exact decomposition is

\[
\boxed{
\chi_{\mathrm{full}}
=
\chi_{\mathrm{cut}}^{(S)}
+
R_S.
}
\]

This identity separates:

- direct/background response;
- coherent sector-mediated response.

The EIT question is then whether \(R_S\) destructively cancels the otherwise absorptive background.

---

## 4. Absorption functional

Because different sign and phase conventions use either \(\operatorname{Re}\chi\) or \(\operatorname{Im}\chi\) as absorption, introduce a real linear absorption functional

\[
\mathcal A[\chi].
\]

For example,

\[
\mathcal A[\chi]=\operatorname{Im}\chi
\]

in one convention, while another normalized response may use

\[
\mathcal A[\chi]=\operatorname{Re}\chi.
\]

Linearity gives

\[
\mathcal A[\chi_{\mathrm{full}}]
=
\mathcal A[\chi_{\mathrm{cut}}^{(S)}]
+
\mathcal A[R_S].
\]

# Part I. General response-theoretic EIT

## 5. Definition of sector-mediated EIT

Let \(\omega_0\) be a real observation frequency such that the response is regular there.

A system exhibits **sector-mediated EIT at \(\omega_0\)** when:

1. the sector-cut system is absorptive,
   \[
   \mathcal A[\chi_{\mathrm{cut}}^{(S)}(\omega_0)]>0;
   \]

2. the selected sector contributes nontrivially,
   \[
   R_S(\omega_0)\neq 0;
   \]

3. the full absorption is reduced,
   \[
   \mathcal A[\chi_{\mathrm{full}}(\omega_0)]
   <
   \mathcal A[\chi_{\mathrm{cut}}^{(S)}(\omega_0)];
   \]

4. the reduction is phase-coherent and disappears when the sector is cut;

5. the feature is not produced solely by resolved pole splitting.

This definition includes both perfect and imperfect EIT.

### Perfect EIT

Perfect sector-mediated EIT occurs when

\[
\mathcal A[\chi_{\mathrm{full}}(\omega_0)]=0.
\]

### Partial EIT

Partial sector-mediated EIT occurs when

\[
0<
\mathcal A[\chi_{\mathrm{full}}(\omega_0)]
<
\mathcal A[\chi_{\mathrm{cut}}^{(S)}(\omega_0)].
\]

---

## 6. General absorption-equivalence theorem

### Theorem 1: Sector-mediated absorption cancellation

Assume

\[
\mathcal A[\chi_{\mathrm{cut}}^{(S)}(\omega_0)]>0.
\]

Then the following statements are equivalent.

### (A) Perfect sector-mediated EIT

\[
\mathcal A[\chi_{\mathrm{full}}(\omega_0)]=0
\]

with

\[
R_S(\omega_0)\neq0.
\]

### (B) Exact cancellation of the absorptive background

\[
\boxed{
\mathcal A[R_S(\omega_0)]
=
-
\mathcal A[\chi_{\mathrm{cut}}^{(S)}(\omega_0)].
}
\]

### (C) Transparency is destroyed by the sector cut

\[
\mathcal A[\chi_{\mathrm{full}}(\omega_0)]=0,
\]

while

\[
\mathcal A[\chi_{\mathrm{cut}}^{(S)}(\omega_0)]>0.
\]

### Proof

Using

\[
\chi_{\mathrm{full}}
=
\chi_{\mathrm{cut}}^{(S)}
+
R_S,
\]

and the linearity of \(\mathcal A\),

\[
\mathcal A[\chi_{\mathrm{full}}]
=
\mathcal A[\chi_{\mathrm{cut}}^{(S)}]
+
\mathcal A[R_S].
\]

Therefore,

\[
\mathcal A[\chi_{\mathrm{full}}(\omega_0)]=0
\]

if and only if

\[
\mathcal A[R_S(\omega_0)]
=
-
\mathcal A[\chi_{\mathrm{cut}}^{(S)}(\omega_0)].
\]

The assumption that the cut response is absorptive ensures that \(R_S\neq0\). The equivalence with statement (C) follows directly from the definition of the sector cut.

\[
\blacksquare
\]

### Physical meaning

The theorem states that EIT is not identified by a vanishing total response alone. It is identified by a nonzero coherent response that cancels an otherwise absorptive background.

## 7. Complex transfer-zero theorem

Absorption cancellation alone may leave a dispersive response. A stronger condition is the vanishing of the full complex response.

Write

\[
\chi_{\mathrm{full}}(\omega)
=
\frac{N_{\mathrm{full}}(\omega)}
{Q_{\mathrm{full}}(\omega)}.
\]

Let \(\omega_0\) be a regular point satisfying

\[
Q_{\mathrm{full}}(\omega_0)\neq0.
\]

### Theorem 2: Complex response-zero equivalence

The following conditions are equivalent.

### (A) Regular full-response zero

\[
\chi_{\mathrm{full}}(\omega_0)=0
\]

and

\[
\chi_{\mathrm{cut}}^{(S)}(\omega_0)\neq0.
\]

### (B) Exact complex cancellation

\[
\boxed{
R_S(\omega_0)
=
-
\chi_{\mathrm{cut}}^{(S)}(\omega_0).
}
\]

### (C) Numerator zero without a pole

\[
N_{\mathrm{full}}(\omega_0)=0,
\]

\[
Q_{\mathrm{full}}(\omega_0)\neq0,
\]

and

\[
\chi_{\mathrm{cut}}^{(S)}(\omega_0)\neq0.
\]

### Proof

From

\[
\chi_{\mathrm{full}}
=
\chi_{\mathrm{cut}}^{(S)}
+
R_S,
\]

one immediately obtains

\[
\chi_{\mathrm{full}}(\omega_0)=0
\iff
R_S(\omega_0)
=
-
\chi_{\mathrm{cut}}^{(S)}(\omega_0).
\]

At a regular point,

\[
\chi_{\mathrm{full}}(\omega_0)=0
\iff
N_{\mathrm{full}}(\omega_0)=0.
\]

\[
\blacksquare
\]

### Proposed general definition

A **perfect response-zero EIT point** is a regular real-frequency zero of the full transfer function that:

1. is induced by the control field;
2. disappears under the physical sector cut;
3. is generated by a nonzero sector response;
4. is not merely a consequence of a pre-existing zero in the direct optical response.

# Part II. Explicit Schur-complement criterion

## 8. Two-lower-state response

For a two-lower-state coherence sector, the exact reduced response is

\[
\Xi
=
S_1
-
\frac{\beta K_{12}K_{21}}
{\gamma_g+\beta S_2},
\]

where

\[
S_a=d_a^\dagger Gd_a,
\]

\[
K_{ab}=d_a^\dagger Gd_b,
\]

\[
G=A^{-1},
\]

and

\[
\beta=\frac{|\Omega_c|^2}{4}.
\]

The direct response is

\[
\Xi_{\mathrm{cut}}^{(S)}=S_1,
\]

and the sector response is

\[
R_S
=
-
\frac{\beta K_{12}K_{21}}
{\gamma_g+\beta S_2}.
\]

---

## 9. Exact response-EIT condition

### Theorem 3: Schur-complement EIT condition

At a regular point where

\[
\gamma_g+\beta S_2\neq0,
\]

the following conditions are equivalent.

### (A) Perfect complex EIT

\[
\Xi=0.
\]

### (B) Exact direct/sector cancellation

\[
R_S=-S_1.
\]

### (C) Kernel identity

\[
\boxed{
S_1(\gamma_g+\beta S_2)
-
\beta K_{12}K_{21}
=
0.
}
\]

### Proof

Substitute the expression for \(\Xi\):

\[
0
=
S_1
-
\frac{\beta K_{12}K_{21}}
{\gamma_g+\beta S_2}.
\]

Multiplying by the nonzero denominator gives

\[
S_1(\gamma_g+\beta S_2)
-
\beta K_{12}K_{21}
=
0.
\]

The relation \(R_S=-S_1\) follows from \(\Xi=S_1+R_S\).

\[
\blacksquare
\]

### Interpretation

The universal EIT condition is therefore not

\[
\ker\Omega\neq0,
\]

but rather

\[
\boxed{
\text{direct optical response}
+
\text{coherent sector response}
=
0.
}
\]

# Part III. Dark states and CPT

## 10. Four distinct notions

The following four concepts must be separated.

### Optical dark vector

\[
\Omega|D\rangle=0.
\]

Equivalently,

\[
|D\rangle\in\ker\Omega.
\]

### Stationary pure Lindblad dark state

A normalized state \(|D\rangle\) satisfying

\[
L_\mu|D\rangle=\lambda_\mu|D\rangle
\]

for all jump operators, together with the effective-Hamiltonian eigenvector condition.

### Coherent population trapping

The steady-state population is trapped in a nonabsorbing coherent state or subspace, typically with

\[
\rho_{ee}^{\mathrm{ss}}=0.
\]

### Observable EIT

A control-induced coherent cancellation in the measured probe response, diagnosed through

\[
R_S,
\]

a response zero or absorption minimum, and pole/residue interference.

In a general multilevel open system, these four notions are not equivalent.

---

## 11. Textbook \(\Lambda\)-system equivalence corollary

Consider an ideal three-level \(\Lambda\) system with:

1. two lower states \(|g_1\rangle,|g_2\rangle\);
2. one excited state \(|e\rangle\);
3. nonzero probe and control couplings;
4. exact two-photon resonance;
5. zero ground-state dephasing;
6. spontaneous emission only from the excited state;
7. weak-probe response;
8. a unique steady state after optical pumping.

### Corollary 1: Textbook dark-state equivalence

Under these assumptions, the following statements are equivalent.

### (i) Hamiltonian dark state

There exists a nonzero state

\[
|D\rangle
\propto
\Omega_c|g_1\rangle
-
\Omega_p|g_2\rangle
\]

such that

\[
\Omega|D\rangle=0
\]

and \(|D\rangle\) is stationary in the rotating frame.

### (ii) Stationary pure Lindblad dark state

\[
\mathcal L(|D\rangle\langle D|)=0.
\]

### (iii) Coherent population trapping

The steady state satisfies

\[
\rho_{\mathrm{ss}}
=
|D\rangle\langle D|,
\]

and

\[
\rho_{ee}^{\mathrm{ss}}=0.
\]

### (iv) Nonzero ground-state coherence

\[
\rho_{g_2g_1}^{\mathrm{ss}}\neq0.
\]

### (v) Exact sector-mediated cancellation

\[
R_S(\omega_0)
=
-
\chi_{\mathrm{cut}}^{(S)}(\omega_0).
\]

### (vi) Perfect probe-response zero

\[
\chi_{\mathrm{full}}(\omega_0)=0.
\]

Therefore,

\[
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
&\text{Hamiltonian dark state}\\
&\Longleftrightarrow
\text{stationary Lindblad dark state}\\
&\Longleftrightarrow
\text{CPT}\\
&\Longleftrightarrow
\text{sector-mediated cancellation}\\
&\Longleftrightarrow
\text{perfect EIT}.
\end{aligned}
}
\]

### Meaning of the corollary

The conventional dark-state interpretation of EIT is correct in the ideal \(\Lambda\) limit.

The proposed theory does not reject this interpretation. It embeds it as a special equivalence class inside a broader response theory.

# Part IV. Breakdown of the textbook equivalence

## 12. General multilevel open systems

Outside the ideal \(\Lambda\) assumptions, the implications separate.

### Dark state without stationary CPT

It is possible that

\[
\ker\Omega\neq\{0\},
\]

while ground-state dephasing or relaxation prevents the dark vector from being a common eigenvector of all jump operators.

Then:

\[
\text{optical dark vector}
\centernot\Rightarrow
\text{stationary pure dark state}.
\]

### Stationary dark state without observable EIT

A dark state or dark subspace may exist, while:

- the probe source has negligible overlap with it;
- the detector is insensitive to it;
- ensemble averaging washes out the feature;
- optical depth is too small;
- another incoherent background dominates.

Then:

\[
\text{dark state}
\centernot\Rightarrow
\text{observable EIT}.
\]

### CPT without a clean transfer zero

Population may accumulate in a weakly absorbing state, but the measured response may remain finite because of other optical channels.

Then:

\[
\text{CPT}
\centernot\Rightarrow
\text{perfect response-zero EIT}.
\]

### EIT without a dark state

It is possible that

\[
\ker\Omega=\{0\},
\]

and no stationary pure dark state exists, while

\[
S_1(\gamma_g+\beta S_2)
-
\beta K_{12}K_{21}
=
0
\]

or approximately vanishes.

Then:

\[
\text{observable coherent transparency}
\centernot\Rightarrow
\text{dark state}.
\]

# Part V. Minimal \(2g+2e\) boundary

## 13. Full-rank \(2g+2e\) obstruction

Let

\[
D=(d_p,d_c)
\]

be the \(2\times2\) dipole matrix, and let \(G\) be an invertible \(2\times2\) excited-state resolvent.

Define

\[
S_p=d_p^\dagger Gd_p,
\]

\[
S_c=d_c^\dagger Gd_c,
\]

\[
K_{pc}=d_p^\dagger Gd_c,
\]

\[
K_{cp}=d_c^\dagger Gd_p.
\]

Then

\[
\boxed{
S_pS_c-K_{pc}K_{cp}
=
|\det D|^2\det G.
}
\]

At ideal two-photon resonance,

\[
\chi_{\mathrm{full}}
=
\frac{S_pS_c-K_{pc}K_{cp}}{S_c}.
\]

Therefore, if

\[
\operatorname{rank}D=2
\]

and \(G\) is regular,

\[
\chi_{\mathrm{full}}\neq0.
\]

### Theorem 4: Minimal exact-zero no-go

A regular ideal \(2g+2e\) system with full-rank optical coupling cannot exhibit a perfect dark-state-free response zero.

Equivalently,

\[
\boxed{
\text{full-rank }2g+2e
\Longrightarrow
\text{no perfect real-axis dark-state-free EIT zero}.
}
\]

However, a complex transfer zero may approach the real axis and produce a deep EIT-like absorption minimum.

### PRL significance

This result identifies the minimal boundary between:

- strong dark-state-free coherent transparency;
- exact dark-state-free EIT.

It suggests that the smallest regular system supporting an exact full-rank dark-state-free EIT zero may require:

- \(2g+3e\);
- a closed-loop coupling;
- correlated dissipation;
- a larger non-normal excited manifold;
- or the full NV excited-state structure.

# Part VI. Distinguishing EIT from ATS

## 14. Why a transparency dip is insufficient

A control field can produce a transparency-like dip through:

1. coherent destructive interference;
2. Autler--Townes splitting;
3. incoherent spectral-hole burning;
4. population redistribution;
5. saturation.

Therefore, a response minimum alone is not a sufficient EIT criterion.

---

## 15. Operational EIT conditions

For a PRL-level claim, the following operational conditions should be required.

### Condition 1: Nonzero sector correction

\[
R_S(\omega_0)\neq0.
\]

### Condition 2: Sector-cut destruction

The transparency is absent or strongly reduced in

\[
\chi_{\mathrm{cut}}^{(S)}.
\]

### Condition 3: Pole/residue interference

The spectral decomposition contains interfering pole contributions with opposite absorptive signs or phases.

### Condition 4: No resolved ATS doublet

The relevant pole separation is smaller than, or not parametrically larger than, the pole widths in the EIT regime.

### Condition 5: Control-induced numerator zero or near-zero

The control field moves a transfer zero toward the physical frequency axis.

### Condition 6: Phase coherence

Phase randomization or dephasing of the selected sector destroys the cancellation.

### Condition 7: Passive background

The transparency is not produced by optical gain.

These conditions define a coherent response phenomenon rather than a generic reduction in absorption.

# Part VII. Integration with the Class I--III no-go theory

## 16. Master-response classification

The master response

\[
R_{S,\Gamma}
\]

is classified by the invariant asymptotic index

\[
\nu[R_S].
\]

### Class I: exact structural no-go

\[
R_{S,\Gamma}\equiv0,
\qquad
\nu=\infty.
\]

The chosen sector cannot contribute to EIT.

### Class II: dissipative suppression

\[
R_{S,\Gamma}
=
\Gamma^{-\nu}r_\nu
+
O(\Gamma^{-\nu-1}),
\qquad
0<\nu<\infty.
\]

The EIT pathway exists but is algebraically suppressed.

### Class III: protected survival

\[
R_{S,\Gamma}
=
r_0
+
O(\Gamma^{-1}),
\qquad
r_0\neq0.
\]

An order-one coherent contribution survives arbitrarily strong scaled dissipation.

---

## 17. Combined EIT/no-go taxonomy

The response-EIT condition and the Class I--III classification answer complementary questions.

### Question 1

Does sector \(S\) contribute to the observable response?

Answered by \(R_S\) and Class I--III.

### Question 2

Does that contribution cancel the absorptive background?

Answered by the response-EIT condition.

Thus,

\[
\boxed{
\text{Class of }R_S
+
\text{relative phase and amplitude against }\chi_{\mathrm{cut}}^{(S)}
=
\text{observable EIT verdict}.
}
\]

A Class III system need not exhibit EIT if the surviving response has the wrong phase.

A Class II system may exhibit EIT at finite \(\Gamma\), despite asymptotic suppression.

A Class I system cannot exhibit EIT through the selected sector.

## 24. Suggested experimental null tests

### Sector dephasing test

Increase the dephasing rate of sector \(S\). A coherent EIT feature should collapse.

### Phase randomization test

Randomize the relative control/probe phase when the geometry is phase sensitive. The coherent cancellation should disappear.

### Sector-cut proxy

Use polarization, magnetic field, or strain to switch off the relevant coupling path while preserving the direct optical line.

### Pole-zero tracking

Track the movement of the transparency zero and poles as a function of control strength.

### ATS crossover

Show that the low-control regime is interference dominated, while the large-control regime crosses into pole splitting.

\propto

in the appropriate Class-II regime.

\[
\boxed{
\text{EIT}
\Longleftrightarrow
\text{control-induced coherent cancellation of an otherwise absorptive observable response}.

\[
\boxed{
\chi_{\mathrm{full}}(\omega_0)=0,

R_S(\omega_0)
=
-\chi_{\mathrm{cut}}^{(S)}(\omega_0),
